# Functionalist Counterarguments to Phenomenological‐Enactivist Claims about Dance and AI

## Dennett’s functionalist view: consciousness as functional organization

### Functionalism in *Consciousness Explained* (1991)

In his early work Dennett defends **functionalism**, the view that mental properties are individuated by their causal and computational roles. In *Consciousness Explained*, he argues that replicating the **functional organization** of a human mind would replicate mental qualities regardless of the material used. Describing a thought‑experiment about a computer that reproduces the functional structure of a wine taster’s cognitive system, he writes that if one “reproduced the functional structure of the wine‑taster’s cognitive system, including all his memory, goals, likes and dislikes,” the resulting system would possess “all the mental properties that matter: his joys and woes, insights and addictions, savoured memories and vices”[andrewmbailey.com](https://andrewmbailey.com/papers/ConsciousnessExplained.pdf#:~:text=grouped%20under%20the%20label%20of,will%20ipso%20facto%20be%20reproduced). Dennett emphasizes that “it doesn’t matter whether the material used is organic molecules or silicon”[andrewmbailey.com](https://andrewmbailey.com/papers/ConsciousnessExplained.pdf#:~:text=says%2C%20whether%20a%20system%20is,mere%20machine%2C%20no%20matter%20how). He concedes that many people think machines cannot truly appreciate art or wine, but he counters that the brain itself is a “magnificent machine,” and if the brain’s control functions can be reproduced in silicon, the resulting system will enjoy and savour wine just as a human does[andrewmbailey.com](https://andrewmbailey.com/papers/ConsciousnessExplained.pdf#:~:text=rageous%20at%20first%20blush,the%20place%20where%20the%20witnessed).

Dennett thus rejects the idea that embodied, organic material (Husserl’s **Leib**) confers a special quality to consciousness. To him, consciousness is a **virtual machine** realized in whatever physical substrate can implement the requisite functional organization.

### Embodiment and silicon in later works

Dennett’s later writings reaffirm this functionalist stance. In a 1994 article on building a conscious robot he notes that while conscious robots may be difficult to construct, there is no reason in principle why consciousness cannot be realized in an assembly of “silicon chips, wires, tiny motors and cameras.” He dismisses vitalistic claims that an inorganic robot must lack a special ingredient: once one concedes materialism, “the powers of organic compounds are mechanistically reducible and hence reproducible … \[so\] there is no serious scientific or philosophical thesis linking its fate to the proposition that a protein‑free conscious robot can be made”[phil415.pbworks.com](https://phil415.pbworks.com/f/DennettPractical.pdf). He does acknowledge practical reasons for embodiment—building robots that operate in the real world exposes problems of real‑time control and prevents designers from overlooking crucial challenges—but he denies that embodiment provides any “special vital juice” absent in simulations[phil415.pbworks.com](https://phil415.pbworks.com/f/DennettPractical.pdf#:~:text=It%20is%20important%20to%20recognize,of%20simulating%20all%20the%20relevant).

In *Sweet Dreams* (2005) Dennett elaborates his view of **broad functionalism**. He characterizes functionalism as the claim that “handsome is as handsome does – that matter matters only because of what matter can do”[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Daniel-Dennett/publication/329650273_Sweet_Dreams_Philosophical_Obstacles_to_a_Science_of_Consciousness/links/5d7a62ad299bf1ec8bd3573b/Sweet-Dreams-Philosophical-Obstacles-to-a-Science-of-Consciousness.pdf#:~:text=Broad%20Functionalism%20and%20Minimalism%20Functionalism,can%20muster%2C%20functionalism%20in%20practice). Science seeks minimal characterizations that generalize across physical media; thus wings need not have feathers to fly, and minds need not be organic to think[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Daniel-Dennett/publication/329650273_Sweet_Dreams_Philosophical_Obstacles_to_a_Science_of_Consciousness/links/5d7a62ad299bf1ec8bd3573b/Sweet-Dreams-Philosophical-Obstacles-to-a-Science-of-Consciousness.pdf#:~:text=than%20one%20might%20have%20thought,the%20maximally%20general%2C%20maximally%20non). Dennett argues that a mind is fundamentally a control system: while our brains are organic, anything that can compute the same control functions “would serve as well”[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Daniel-Dennett/publication/329650273_Sweet_Dreams_Philosophical_Obstacles_to_a_Science_of_Consciousness/links/5d7a62ad299bf1ec8bd3573b/Sweet-Dreams-Philosophical-Obstacles-to-a-Science-of-Consciousness.pdf). The bold functionalist vision—what he elsewhere calls *computationalism* or *strong AI*—asserts that “in principle you could replace your wet, organic brain with a bunch of silicon chips and wires and go right on thinking (and being conscious)”[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Daniel-Dennett/publication/329650273_Sweet_Dreams_Philosophical_Obstacles_to_a_Science_of_Consciousness/links/5d7a62ad299bf1ec8bd3573b/Sweet-Dreams-Philosophical-Obstacles-to-a-Science-of-Consciousness.pdf#:~:text=the%20,being%20conscious%2C%20and%20so%20forth). Even when neuroscientists discover that specific neurochemical or anatomical details matter, Dennett insists that they matter **only** because of their functional roles; functionalism is vindicated because these details are important precisely as components of the overall computational organization[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Daniel-Dennett/publication/329650273_Sweet_Dreams_Philosophical_Obstacles_to_a_Science_of_Consciousness/links/5d7a62ad299bf1ec8bd3573b/Sweet-Dreams-Philosophical-Obstacles-to-a-Science-of-Consciousness.pdf#:~:text=This%20has%20left%20the%20mistaken,What%20those%20molecules%20do%20turns).

In summary, Dennett’s functionalism treats consciousness as a computationally realizable pattern. Bodily matter (Körper/Leib) has no intrinsic role beyond implementing the functional architecture; therefore, any device that reproduces the functional organization of dance cognition would, on Dennett’s view, possess the same experiential properties.

## Putnam’s functionalism and multiple realizability

### Turing‑machine functionalism

Hilary Putnam introduced **functionalism** in the 1960s and 1970s as an alternative to type‑identity theories. In “The Nature of Mental States” he models an organism as a **probabilistic automaton** and defines its **functional organization** in terms of transition probabilities between total states and sensory inputs[home.csulb.edu](https://home.csulb.edu/~cwallis/382/readings/482/putnam.nature.mental.states.pdf). Mental states are characterized by their place in this functional organization rather than by their physical realization. Putnam articulates the **functional‑state hypothesis**: organisms capable of pain are probabilistic automata with at least one **Description** whose total state corresponds to feeling pain; the physical parts do not themselves have such a Description[home.csulb.edu](https://home.csulb.edu/~cwallis/382/readings/482/putnam.nature.mental.states.pdf#:~:text=i,inputs%20are%20in%20that%20subset). Because functional descriptions abstract away from physical details, mental kinds are not identical to specific brain states.

Putnam argues that mental states are **multiply realizable**. For the brain‑state identity theory to be true, there would have to be a single physical‑chemical state common to all species capable of pain—mammals, reptiles, octopi, etc.—and correlated with each occurrence of pain. Comparative neuroanatomy and evolution make this highly implausible[home.csulb.edu](https://home.csulb.edu/~cwallis/382/readings/482/putnam.nature.mental.states.pdf). He notes that it is overwhelmingly probable that psychological predicates like “hungry” apply to both mammals and octopi but have different physical‑chemical correlates[home.csulb.edu](https://home.csulb.edu/~cwallis/382/readings/482/putnam.nature.mental.states.pdf). The conclusion is that **mental kinds can be realized by distinct physical kinds**, so no mental state is identical to any one physical state. The Stanford Encyclopedia explains that, because of multiple realizability, early identity theorists’ requirement that each mental kind correspond to a neural kind is “very probably false” and that even silicon‑based androids or Martians with “green slime” could realize mental states[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2019/entries/multiple-realizability/#:~:text=In%20a%20series%20of%20papers,all%20speak%20against%20this%20requirement). Putnam’s canonical argument states: (1) all mental kinds are multiply realizable by distinct physical kinds; (2) if a mental kind is multiply realizable, it cannot be identical to any specific physical kind; therefore, (3) no mental kind is identical to a physical kind[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2019/entries/multiple-realizability/#:~:text=Stated%20in%20canonical%20form%2C%20Putnam%27s,theory%20conclusion%20from%20two%20premises).

Putnam later extended multiple realizability into a critique of **computational functionalism**. He argued that mental kinds are not only compositionally plastic (realizable in different materials) but also **computationally plastic**—the same mental state can be implemented by different computational processes. Thus there is no unique abstract algorithm for “pain”; the functional roles themselves can vary across systems. Multiple realizability therefore challenges reductionist attempts to model human experience—such as complex kinesthetic skills in dance—in a single computational framework.

## The strongest functionalist objection to a phenomenological thesis about dance

A phenomenological–enactivist thesis holds that dance practice involves embodied qualities grounded in the **lived body (Leib)**. The Husserlian distinction between **Körper** (objective body) and **Leib** (lived, experiential body) emphasizes that kinesthetic sensations, proprioceptive awareness, and affective attunement are integral to dance and resistant to automation. Functionalists challenge this thesis by denying that embodiment adds anything essential to mental states. The strongest version of the functionalist objection can be formulated as follows:

1. **Mental states are individuated by functional roles, not by their physical substrates.** Consciousness and kinesthetic experience are defined by patterns of causal relations between inputs, internal states and outputs. Dennett argues that if one reproduces the functional organization of a human wine‑taster, one reproduces all his mental properties[andrewmbailey.com](https://andrewmbailey.com/papers/ConsciousnessExplained.pdf#:~:text=grouped%20under%20the%20label%20of,will%20ipso%20facto%20be%20reproduced). Similarly, Putnam’s functionalism insists that “pain” is a functional state characterized by its role in a probabilistic automaton[home.csulb.edu](https://home.csulb.edu/~cwallis/382/readings/482/putnam.nature.mental.states.pdf#:~:text=i,inputs%20are%20in%20that%20subset). The multiple‑realizability premise holds that mental kinds can be realized by different physical structures[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2019/entries/multiple-realizability/#:~:text=In%20a%20series%20of%20papers,all%20speak%20against%20this%20requirement).

2. **Embodied processes can be simulated or implemented in alternative media.** Dennett argues that there is no principled reason why a conscious system cannot be built from silicon, wires and motors; any assembly that replicates the control functions of the organic brain would suffice[phil415.pbworks.com](https://phil415.pbworks.com/f/DennettPractical.pdf#:~:text=It%20is%20unlikely%2C%20in%20my,that%20is%20requisite%20for%20consciousness). In his later work he contends that a mind is essentially a control system; in principle one could replace one’s “wet, organic brain with a bunch of silicon chips and wires and go right on thinking”[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Daniel-Dennett/publication/329650273_Sweet_Dreams_Philosophical_Obstacles_to_a_Science_of_Consciousness/links/5d7a62ad299bf1ec8bd3573b/Sweet-Dreams-Philosophical-Obstacles-to-a-Science-of-Consciousness.pdf). Putnam notes that creatures with different biochemistry (silicon‑based androids, Martians with “green slime”) could realize pain[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2019/entries/multiple-realizability/#:~:text=possessing%20that%20physical,brain).

3. **Therefore, embodied dance experiences are functionally reproducible.** Dance involves complex sensorimotor patterns, but functionalists would argue that these patterns can be described as information‑processing routines: sensory inputs (muscle spindle feedback, vestibular signals), internal states (motor programs, kinesthetic representations) and outputs (movements). An artificial system equipped with appropriate sensors and effectors could instantiate the same functional organization and thus the same kinesthetic awareness. If the “quality” of dance experience amounts to functional relations among sensory inputs, internal memory and motor outputs, then there is nothing in principle preventing generative AI from producing dance performances and even experiencing them. Appeals to the lived body are, on this view, appeals to a particular implementation; what matters is the abstract causal structure, not whether it is realized in flesh or silicon. Functionalists could concede that our current AI systems lack the complexity of human bodies but maintain that this is an engineering limitation, not a metaphysical barrier.

The objection concludes that phenomenological arguments overstate the irreducibility of embodiment. If dance’s experiential qualities supervene solely on functional organization, then sufficiently sophisticated artificial systems could, in principle, replicate them, challenging the claim that dance cannot be automated.

## Phenomenological and enactivist responses to functionalism

Phenomenological and enactivist philosophers challenge functionalism on both conceptual and empirical grounds. Their responses emphasize that mental life is constituted by the lived body, not merely implemented by it, and that functional descriptions fail to capture the normative, affective and experiential dimensions of consciousness.

### Enactive autonomy and the body as self‑individuating

Evan Thompson and Ezequiel Di Paolo argue that the enactive approach differs from other embodied theories by focusing on **autonomy**—the idea that cognitive systems are self‑individuating living beings. Rather than being conventionally individuated like machines, living bodies maintain and reproduce themselves; the body “is not just a locus for solving pragmatic problems but a self‑individuating centre of activity”[evanthompson.me](https://evanthompson.me/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/9780415623612c07.pdf#:~:text=practical%20use,adequate%20account%20of%20how%20the). Thompson notes that the enactive approach thematizes the question *what is a body?*; understanding minds requires understanding living bodies, which actively maintain their own boundaries[upaya.org](https://www.upaya.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Di-Paolo-The-enactive-conception-of-life-2018.pdf#:~:text=with%20different%20facets,a%20line%20of%20theorizing%20that). This emphasis on self‑production and normativity distinguishes enactivism from functionalism, which identifies cognitive systems by their computational organization rather than by their biological autonomy.

Thompson further argues that living structures are **ontologically emergent**: they form a “new order of nature that is qualitatively distinct from the merely physical order”[frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.539841/full#:~:text=cognitive%20systems,Such%20a). Autonomous organisms actively regulate their interactions with the environment through **needful freedom**, simultaneously depending on the environment and exercising agentive control[frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.539841/full#:~:text=purpose%20of%20the%20organism%E2%80%99s%20own,while%20%E2%80%9Cfreedom%E2%80%9D%20expresses%20its%20agentive). Such autonomy grounds agency and cognition in biological embodiment; a “genuine agent is biologically embodied”[frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.539841/full#:~:text=capabilities%20of%20the%20lived%20body,embodiment%3A%20%E2%80%9Ca%20genuine%20agent%20is). On this view, the lived body is not a replaceable implementation but the **source of sense‑making**—the organism enacts a world of significance through its embodied activity. Generative AI systems lack this self‑maintaining, value‑laden engagement with the environment.

### Lived body and phenomenological self‑awareness

Phenomenologists highlight the **pre‑reflective self‑awareness** inherent in perception. Dan Zahavi points out that we are aware of perceptual objects “by being aware of our own body and how the two interact,” and that we cannot perceive physical objects without an accompanying bodily self‑awareness, whether explicit or tacit[frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.539841/full#:~:text=perceives%20it,So%2C%20while%20the%20reciprocity%20of). Husserl emphasises the mutual codependency of world and lived body: the body is both an agent acting in the world and an object perceived; it recedes from explicit attention yet remains a “passively active substratum”[frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.539841/full#:~:text=absent%2C%20or%2C%20as%20Husserl%20would,is%2C%20rather%2C%20a%20different%20mode). This **operant intentionality** or **kinesthetic awareness** underlies skilled actions like dance; it cannot be captured by third‑person functional descriptions because it concerns how the world is experienced from the first‑person perspective.

Gallagher and Zahavi argue that functionalism encouraged the belief that cognition could be instantiated in a **disembodied computer program**, but the rise of embodied cognition showed that mental life is deeply rooted in the body[api.pageplace.de](https://api.pageplace.de/preview/DT0400.9781000202816_A39737698/preview-9781000202816_A39737698.pdf#:~:text=ued%20to%20plague%20the%20cognitive,their%20objections%20to%20disembodied%20cognition). They insist that starting from lived experience allows one to avoid the metaphysical abstractions of functionalism[api.pageplace.de](https://api.pageplace.de/preview/DT0400.9781000202816_A39737698/preview-9781000202816_A39737698.pdf#:~:text=2004%3B%20Kim%202005%3B%20Braddon,is%20no%20coincidence%20that%20Edmund). Shaun Gallagher’s physical grounding hypothesis (PGH) further contends that the contents and operations of cognition are grounded in an agent’s physical properties and embodied experience; thus some mental states cannot be adequately accounted for by their functional roles alone[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/246392739_How_the_Body_Shapes_the_Mind#:~:text=substantive%20claim%20of%20the%20EC,PGH). Embodied cognition is compatible with physicalism but not with functionalism, because **implementation matters**: cognition supervenes on many different material and relational facts, not just on abstract causal roles[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/246392739_How_the_Body_Shapes_the_Mind#:~:text=explanation%20of%20cognition,is%20arguably%20compatible%20with%20both).

### Sensorimotor enactivism

Alva Noë and J. Kevin O’Regan argue that **perception is not the construction of internal representations but a skilled form of action**. Seeing consists in actively exploring the environment and mastering **sensorimotor contingencies**—regularities governing how sensory input changes with movement[rctn.org](https://www.rctn.org/vs265/noe-oregan.pdf#:~:text=is%20made%20here,advantage%20of%20this%20approach%20is). Perceivers do not build internal models but rely on the world itself as its own best model. The enactive approach holds that perceiving an object requires not only sensory stimulation but **implicit knowledge of how stimulations vary with bodily movement**; vision depends on appreciation of the sensory effects of movement[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/win2018/entries/action-perception/#:~:text=The%20enactive%20approach%20defended%20by,life%E2%80%9D%2C%20O%E2%80%99Regan%20and%20No%C3%AB%20write). Noë insists that the “perspectival aspect” of perception marks the place of action[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/win2018/entries/action-perception/#:~:text=as%20a%20function%20of%20her,Rather%2C%20vision). Such sensorimotor know‑how is acquired through embodied engagement and cannot be reduced to abstract functional roles. A generative AI that lacks embodied interaction with the world will not have the same sensorimotor mastery and thus cannot reproduce the lived phenomenology of dance.

### Critique of embodied but computationalist theories

Di Paolo cautions that the term **embodied** has been diluted by computationalists who merely add bodily modules to disembodied theories; true embodiment requires clarifying what role bodies play beyond serving as interfaces[upaya.org](https://www.upaya.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Di-Paolo-The-enactive-conception-of-life-2018.pdf#:~:text=Tenuous%20conceptual%20connections%20with%20the,to%20researchers%20in%20the%20computational). He argues that many supposed embodied approaches remain “veiled versions of computationalism,” and insists that any embodied theory must answer two questions: **What is its conception of bodies? What central role do bodies play that differs from traditional computationalism?**[upaya.org](https://www.upaya.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Di-Paolo-The-enactive-conception-of-life-2018.pdf#:~:text=Tenuous%20conceptual%20connections%20with%20the,play%20in%20this%20theory%20different). Functionalist models that treat the body as an interchangeable sensorimotor platform fail this test because they disregard the organism’s self‑individuating and affective dimensions.

## Reconciling functionalism and phenomenology: acknowledging the challenge

A phenomenological thesis on dance can acknowledge the functionalist challenge without relinquishing the importance of the lived body. One strategy is to adopt a **moderate or embodied functionalism**. Andy Clark has argued for **extended functionalism**, which maintains that mental states are individuated by functional roles yet recognizes that those roles are partly realized through an organism’s embodiment and coupling with the environment. From this perspective, the Leib is **constitutive** of certain functions: kinesthetic perception, balance and affective attunement are not merely inputs but ingredients of the functional organization itself. Thus, a complete functional description of dance must include the bodily processes that make dance possible. Functionalism then becomes a *constraint* rather than a reductive thesis: any artificial system would need to replicate not only abstract causal roles but also the organismic processes of self‑maintenance, normativity and sensorimotor skill acquisition.

Another option is **neurophenomenology**, proposed by Varela, which seeks reciprocal constraints between phenomenological accounts of lived experience and functional/neuroscientific models. Thompson and Varela recommend using first‑person methods to inform functional models, thereby avoiding the **explanatory gap** that arises when functionalists ignore lived experience.

Finally, a phenomenological thesis on dance might embrace **pluralism** about realization: it can concede that mental states are multiply realizable while insisting that certain experiential qualities—such as the affective resonance and intercorporeal attunement of dance—are shaped by our biological bodies and histories. Putnam himself later noted that multiple realizability makes reductionism “empirically untenable”[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2019/entries/multiple-realizability/#:~:text=In%20a%20series%20of%20papers,all%20speak%20against%20this%20requirement). This leaves room for the claim that while a rudimentary functional organization could in principle be realized by machines, **the richness and meaning of human dance is inseparable from the living body**. Generative AI might simulate movements but lacks the bodily self‑individuation, affective tone and intersubjective reciprocity that give dance its distinctive experiential quality. Acknowledging functionalism thus motivates careful analysis of which aspects of dance can be functionally reproduced and which depend on the phenomenological reality of the Leib.

## Conclusion

Dennett and Putnam articulate powerful functionalist arguments: consciousness and kinesthetic experience are defined by functional organization and are multiply realizable across physical media[andrewmbailey.com](https://andrewmbailey.com/papers/ConsciousnessExplained.pdf#:~:text=grouped%20under%20the%20label%20of,will%20ipso%20facto%20be%20reproduced)[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2019/entries/multiple-realizability/#:~:text=In%20a%20series%20of%20papers,all%20speak%20against%20this%20requirement). Dennett argues that one could replace the human brain with silicon chips and preserve consciousness[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Daniel-Dennett/publication/329650273_Sweet_Dreams_Philosophical_Obstacles_to_a_Science_of_Consciousness/links/5d7a62ad299bf1ec8bd3573b/Sweet-Dreams-Philosophical-Obstacles-to-a-Science-of-Consciousness.pdf), and Putnam’s multiple realizability denies that mental kinds are identical to biological states[home.csulb.edu](https://home.csulb.edu/~cwallis/382/readings/482/putnam.nature.mental.states.pdf). From this perspective, AI that replicates the functional patterns of dance could, in principle, match human experience.

Phenomenological and enactivist thinkers respond that consciousness is not merely implemented but constituted by the lived body. The body is a self‑individuating, autonomous system that enacts a world of meaning; perception depends on sensorimotor mastery; and we are always tacitly aware of our embodied agency[frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.539841/full#:~:text=perceives%20it,So%2C%20while%20the%20reciprocity%20of)[frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.539841/full#:~:text=cognitive%20systems,Such%20a). These dimensions are overlooked by functionalism. A thesis on dance can acknowledge the functionalist challenge by specifying the functional roles played by kinesthetic and affective processes while insisting that these roles are inseparable from biological embodiment. Rather than rejecting functional descriptions, phenomenologists can integrate them with first‑person accounts to show why the **Leib** matters for dance and why generative AI, lacking a living body, can only approximate, not duplicate, the lived experience of dancing.
