
# Epistemologies of Artistic Research: Frameworks, Methodologies, and the Theoretical-Practical Divide in Dance Studies

The integration of artistic practices into Higher Education Institutions (HEIs) over the past three decades has precipitated a profound epistemological paradigm shift within the global academy (Nelson, 2013).1 As formerly free-standing conservatoires and specialized arts schools were systematically drawn into or absorbed by the broader university sector, the foundational definitions of what constitutes legitimate "research," what serves as valid "evidence," and how "knowledge" is produced and disseminated were subjected to intense structural scrutiny and necessary renegotiation (Nelson, 2013).1 This institutional friction has given rise to robust methodological frameworks designed to articulate how art-making functions not merely as personal expression, but as a rigorous, verifiable investigative process. Within this evolving academic landscape, the methodological debate frequently centers on the inherent tension between practice-based methodologies—where the artwork and the creative process themselves are central to the inquiry—and traditional theoretical-conceptual approaches, which analyze the arts from a position of established academic distance.

The necessity to legitimize the arts within the academy is not merely an intellectual exercise; it is intrinsically tied to neoliberal research imperatives, funding distributions, and the structural hierarchies of university degree-awarding powers, particularly at the doctoral level (Nelson, 2013).1This has led to the development of sophisticated paradigms that attempt to map the contours of artistic epistemology. This exhaustive report examines the foundational frameworks that currently govern artistic research, focusing critically on Henk Borgdorff’s tripartite taxonomy presented in The Conflict of the Faculties: Perspectives on Artistic Research and Academia(Borgdorff, 2012) and Robin Nelson’s multi-mode epistemological model detailed in Practice as Research in the Arts: Principles, Protocols, Pedagogies, Resistances (Nelson, 2013).

Building upon these macroscopic theoretical frameworks, the analysis subsequently transitions into a highly specific, fundamentally problematic domain for traditional research: dance studies. By evaluating the distinct affordances and inherent limitations of practice-based versus theoretical-conceptual approaches to dance, this report elucidates the precise conditions under which a purely theoretical investigation of an inherently embodied, ephemeral art form is not only methodologically legitimate but academically indispensable for producing specific modes of knowledge.

## The Institutional Matrix: Henk Borgdorff’s Taxonomy of Artistic Research

In The Conflict of the Faculties: Perspectives on Artistic Research and Academia (Borgdorff, 2012), Henk Borgdorff addresses the deep structural, institutional, and philosophical schisms that have historically separated academic research from artistic practice (Borgdorff, 2012).3 The title itself deliberately invokes Immanuel Kant's historical treatises on the structural organization of knowledge, arguing forcefully that the "faculties of the human mind are not subject to a value hierarchy" (Borgdorff, 2012).3 Therefore, Borgdorff contends, the institutional faculties in which these human capacities are challenged and utilized possess an inherent, democratic right to equal treatment, which necessitates the establishment of properly funded, practice-based Master's and PhD programs in the arts (Borgdorff, 2012).3

Borgdorff’s central thesis operates simultaneously on multiple epistemological dimensions: it is constative in its description of the current landscape, interpretive in its philosophical analysis, and profoundly performative in its ultimate intent (Borgdorff, 2012).4 By actively articulating the parameters, vocabularies, and justifications of artistic research, the text actively causes the practices involved to manifest themselves in specific, academically legible ways, thereby setting them into motion and engaging directly with the power dynamics between academia and the contemporary art world (Borgdorff, 2012).4To clarify this emerging phenomenon and resolve the boundary disputes regarding what constitutes legitimate academic inquiry, Borgdorff delineates three distinct methodological perspectives regarding the relationship between research and art practice: research onthe arts, research forthe arts, and research in the arts (Borgdorff, 2012).7

## Research on the Arts (The Interpretive Perspective)

Research on the arts constitutes investigations where art practice, considered in the broadest sense of the word, serves strictly as the primary object of study (Borgdorff, 2012).8 This mode is characterized by an "interpretive perspective," a stance that is entirely common to, and expected by, the established research traditions of the humanities and the social sciences (Borgdorff, 2012).9Methodologically, research on the arts demands a fundamental "theoretical distance" between the researcher and the artistic object or event (Borgdorff, 2012).8 The researcher observes, analyzes, contextualizes, and interprets the artistic phenomenon from an external vantage point, seeking to draw valid, verifiable conclusions about the practice, its historical development, its sociological impact, or its underlying aesthetic value (Borgdorff, 2012).7

In this traditional paradigm, the definition of what constitutes art, how it functions in society, and what meanings it generates is determined almost exclusively by the theoretical domain of the observing discipline, whether that be musicology, art history, cultural sociology, or performance studies (Borgdorff, 2012).7 The artwork is rendered as an artifact, a historical text, or a cultural symptom to be systematically decoded. Crucially, while art practice is the undeniable subject matter of the research, it is neither the methodological engine driving the inquiry nor the final outcome of the study (Borgdorff, 2012).9 The outcome is invariably a traditional academic output, such as a textual thesis, a historical monograph, or a peer-reviewed journal article. This approach does not require the researcher to possess artistic skills, as the primary tools of investigation are archival research, theoretical discourse, and critical analysis.

## Research for the Arts (The Instrumental Perspective)

The second category within Borgdorff's taxonomy, research for the arts, adopts an "instrumental perspective" (Borgdorff, 2012).9 This mode is fundamentally a form of applied research; its primary, driving objective is to serve, facilitate, or structurally enhance artistic practice (Borgdorff, 2012).9Research for the arts typically involves the systematic development of new materials, specialized tools, innovative technologies, or physical applications that artists can subsequently utilize in their own independent creative endeavors (Borgdorff, 2012).7

Practical examples of this instrumental mode include investigations into the acoustic properties of new metallic alloys for brass instrument making, the programming of complex new algorithms for digital choreography and motion capture, or the advanced chemical analysis of historical pigments to ensure the longevity of contemporary painting. In these instances, the research is conducted strictly in the service of the art form, providing pragmatic, objective, and often highly scientific knowledge that expands the technical and material boundaries of what artists can achieve in their respective mediums. Much like research on the arts, the research process itself is not the art; rather, it provides the vital infrastructural, technological, or material foundation upon which subsequent art can be constructed and realized.

## Research in the Arts (The Performative and Practice-Based Perspective)

The most radical, transformative, and hotly debated category in Borgdorff’s entire framework is research inthe arts—a paradigm often referred to interchangeably in the literature as research through the arts or, most broadly, as artistic research (Borgdorff, 2012).7Borgdorff argues forcefully that this specific mode of inquiry is of equal epistemological and academic value to research on the arts, and must therefore be treated with absolute parity at the institutional level (Borgdorff, 2012).3

In this performative paradigm, the art practice is absolutely paramount; it serves simultaneously as the subject matter, the primary method, the contextual environment, and the ultimate outcome of the research (Borgdorff, 2012).9 The creative practice—whether it occurs in the painter's studio, the sculptor's atelier, or the dancer's rehearsal space—is not merely the motivating factor for the study, but is demonstrably central to the research process itself (Borgdorff, 2012).9 Methodologically speaking, the creative process forms the active pathway through which new insights, novel understandings, and innovative products come into being (Borgdorff, 2012).9

Borgdorff explicitly links this methodological approach to Donald Schön’s foundational concept of "reflection-in-action," positioning the investigating artists as "reflective practitioners" who engage in complex hermeneutic and experimental methods to reveal, interrogate, and articulate the tacit knowledge that is deeply situated and embodied within specific artworks and creative processes (Borgdorff, 2012).7A major distinguishing feature of research in the arts is the deliberate, necessary embrace of uncertainty regarding the final outcome, a characteristic that sharply deviates from the rigid expectations of pragmatic, objective scientific knowledge (Borgdorff, 2012).7 Furthermore, the context for this research is intrinsically dual-facing: it must hold rigorous significance within the academic forum while concurrently existing as a meaningful, impactful contribution to the contemporary world of art (Borgdorff, 2012).9Consequently, the outcomes of research in the arts are intrinsically hybrid in nature, producing new theoretical insights alongside tangible, experiential artworks, installations, and live performances (Borgdorff, 2012).9

| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Research on the arts | Interpretive / Critical | The primary object of study; analyzed from a theoretical distance. | Traditional academic texts, monographs, historical or sociological analyses. |
| Research for the arts | Instrumental / Applied | The beneficiary of the study; practice is enhanced by the findings. | New tools, software, materials, algorithms, or technological applications. |
| Research in the arts | Performative / Practice-Based | Serves simultaneously as the subject, method, context, and outcome. | Artworks, live performances, and exhibitions, paired with articulated new insights. |

## Praxis and Multi-Mode Epistemologies: Robin Nelson’s Practice as Research Framework

While Henk Borgdorff provides a macroscopic, institutional, and highly philosophical taxonomy to justify the existence of artistic research, Robin Nelson’s seminal text Practice as Research in the Arts: Principles, Protocols, Pedagogies, Resistances (Nelson, 2013) offers a highly granular, methodological blueprint for actively navigating the academy's stringent demands (Nelson, 2013).11 Nelson specifically addresses the "Practice as Research" (PaR) initiative, a model primarily of Anglophone and Nordic origin that explicitly conceptualizes practice or praxis as a primary generator of knowledge, requiring deep critical reflection, situated conceptual integration, and highly documented communication of processes (Nelson, 2013).12

Nelson's comprehensive framework is fundamentally a pragmatic response to the "performance turn" in the humanities and the complex structural integration of free-standing artschools and conservatoires into university HEIs (Nelson, 2013).1 He notes that this institutional shift requires a painful but necessary adjustment of qualification frameworks, curriculum structures, and pedagogic practices to embrace a research dimension that fully satisfies institutional auditing, funding bodies, and examination standards (Nelson, 2013).1 To achieve this epistemological bridging, Nelson advocates for a conceptual migration from a traditional foundation of "hard facts" toward a more flexible paradigm of "liquid knowing," a concept that actively acknowledges the fluid, iterative, messy, and deeply experiential nature of artistic discovery (Nelson, 2013).2

## The Absolute Necessity of Practice-Based Approaches

Nelson articulates specific, rigorous conditions under which practice-based approaches are not merely a creative alternative, but an absolute epistemological necessity for producing valid research. The core argument rests on a vital philosophical distinction between "knowledge"—a noun that suggests a static, bounded, commodified object completely separate from the observer—and "knowing"—a present participle that actively acknowledges a human subject engaged dynamically in the ongoing act of inquiry (Nelson, 2013).2

Practice-based approaches become fundamentally necessary when the articulation of the specific research inquiry must be conducted through the material reality and physical resistance of the medium itself (Nelson, 2013).2 Nelson frequently employs the metaphor of "riding a bicycle" to illustrate the concept of "practical," procedural, or "tacit" knowledge (Nelson, 2013).11 A researcher simply cannot adequately capture, transfer, or interrogate the methodology of riding a bicycle purely through textual description; the practitioner possesses a vague, conscious awareness of some cognitive processes, but the vast majority of the vital "doing-thinking" occurs sub-consciously, kinesthetically, and physically in the moment of action (Nelson, 2013).2 PaR is strictly necessary to capture this active "doing-thinking" through the act of material engagement, addressing specific research motives that seek to find things out through exploratory dynamics inherent to artistic inquiry (Nelson, 2013).2

Furthermore, practice is absolutely necessary when evaluating variables that can only manifest in a real-world, temporal, spatial, and experiential setting. For example, Nelson cites the scenario of evaluating the complex aesthetic, emotional, and visual effects of modern LED lighting on an audience's overall experience of a classic theatrical production like Oklahoma (Nelson, 2013).11 This specific research question cannot be entirely theorized, modeled on a computer, or debated in a seminar room; it must be materialized, staged, and practiced in front of live bodies (Nelson, 2013).11 In such instances, the artistic practice functions as the "principal research activity," and the material outcomes (the live performance, the choreography, the film) are submitted as the "substantial evidence" of the research inquiry (Nelson, 2013).2Nelson emphasizes heavily that in the PaR paradigm, the artwork is not a mere byproduct or illustration of the research; it is the research, harboring embodied, material insights that text alone would fundamentally distort, reduce, or completely lose (Nelson, 2013).2

## The Appropriateness of Theoretical and Conceptual Approaches within PaR

Despite prioritizing the physical art object as substantial evidence of inquiry, Nelson forcefully argues against the romanticized notion that "straight practice" alone is sufficient to qualify as academic research (Nelson, 2013).2 He outlines highly specific conditions where theoretical, conceptual, and textual approaches are entirely appropriate, and indeed structurally necessary, to transition raw practice into verifiable, defensible academic research.

First and foremost, theoretical approaches provide the essential "academic respectability" and protocol required by university degree-awarding bodies, particularly at the PhD level (Nelson, 2013).2 They serve to consciously build the "conceptual framework" for the research, ensuring that the isolated artistic practice is rigorously grounded in established or emerging academic discourses (Nelson, 2013).2 Without a clearly articulated theoretical framework, practice exists in a solipsistic vacuum; theory grounds the work in a recognizable scholarly paradigm, demonstrating how the physical practice speaks to larger intellectual currents like post-structuralism, feminism, or the "performative turn" (Nelson, 2013).2

Second, textual and theoretical approaches are absolutely necessary for "critical reflection" and for intentionally "locating practice in a lineage of influences" (Nelson, 2013).2 An artist operating in the academy must be able to articulate precisely where their work sits historically and aesthetically in relation to their peers, competitors, and historical predecessors. Nelson refers to this necessary theoretical framing as conducting a "practice review"—an artistic alternative to a traditional scientific literature review, where the practitioner-researcher rigorously explores the applied outworkings of other creators in the field to orient themselves and unearth practices similar to their own (Nelson, 2013).14

Third, theoretical approaches are vital for the complex tasks of documentation and evidencing (Nelson, 2013).2 Nelson draws a highly critical distinction between the mere "documentation of a practice" (which is simply an archival record of an event, like a wide-shot video of a play) and the "documentation of a research inquiry" (which actively tracks the underlying methodology, the conceptual failures, the iterative choices, and the shifting hypotheses) (Nelson, 2013).2 Theoretical writing serves as a crucial "translation" that bridges the epistemological gap between the elusive, ephemeral performance and the rigid, permanent demands of academic assessment, allowing the researcher to tell a "fuller story" of the performance than the practice alone could ever provide (Nelson, 2013).2

## The Synthesis: Praxis and the Multi-Mode Epistemological Model

Nelson ultimately resolves the institutional tension between practice and theory through the deployment of a "multi-mode epistemological model" designed to effectively "house the mix" of diverse research methods (Nelson, 2013).2 He argues vehemently against treating practice and theory as mutually exclusive binaries, advocating instead for their deep integration to maximize the research's rigorous contribution to academic knowledge (Nelson, 2013).2 This multi-mode model consists of three interconnected, equally vital spheres of knowing:

1. Know-how: The highly practical, procedural skill, tacit knowledge, and physical mastery possessed by the practitioner (e.g., the dancer's ingrained physical technique, the painter's brush control) (Nelson, 2013).2
2. Know-what: The conscious "reflection-in-action," the intelligent practice, and the active material thinking that occurs sequentially during the creative studio process (Nelson, 2013).2
3. Know-that: The propositional knowledge, theoretical context, historical facts, and academic lineage articulated through textual frameworks (Nelson, 2013).2

By simultaneously mobilizing all three modes of evidence, the practitioner-researcher engages in true praxis—a term defined by Nelson as the deep "imbrications of theory within practice" (Nelson, 2013).2 This synthesized approach renders the previously rigid institutional binary porous, proving conclusively that insightful, rigorous thought is engaged materially in the studio just as effectively as it is engaged abstractly in the mind (Nelson, 2013).2 Consequently, a standard, successful PaR doctoral submission is rarely just an artwork; it is a tripartite entity comprising a durable product (the live performance or exhibition), extensive documentation of the iterative process (sketchbooks, video diaries), and a substantial piece of "complementary writing" that firmly frames the conceptual inquiry and lineage (Nelson, 2013).11

| Epistemological Mode in PaR | Conceptual Description | Concrete Manifestation in a PaR Submission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Know-how | Tacit, procedural, and deeply embodied physical skills. | The flawless physical execution of the artwork; technical virtuosity. |
| Know-what | Reflection-in-action; iterative material thinking. | The documented choices, failures, and pivots made in the studio. |
| Know-that | Propositional, factual knowledge; theoretical framing. | Complementary writing; conceptual frameworks; the "practice review." |

## The Epistemological Problem of Dance: Embodied Practice vs. Theoretical Distance

The methodological debates so clearly articulated by Borgdorff and Nelson reach their absolute highest pitch when applied specifically to the field of dance studies. Dance presents a unique, almost intractable epistemological conundrum for the traditional academy: it is an inherently ephemeral, temporal, and profoundly embodied art form (Adshead-Lansdale, 1997).16 As an object of rigorous study, dance violently resists the permanence of the written word and the static, archivable nature of visual artifacts. Consequently, the specific methodology chosen by a scholar to investigate dance deeply, irrevocably influences what kind of knowledge is ultimately produced, legitimized, and disseminated.

## The Phenomenological Case for Practice-Based Methodologies in Dance

Fierce advocates for practice-based research in dance argue that prioritizing purely theoretical-conceptual approaches inherently marginalizes, and often actively erases, the very physical essence of the medium. The underlying philosophy of arts practice research in this domain posits that experiential learning and knowledge creation through direct physical experience are critical, irreplaceable modes of pedagogical connection, discovery, and application (Overby et al., 2019).18 As scholars frequently point out, dance-based researchers must acknowledge the "body as a place of knowing," challenging the Cartesian dualism that separates the active mind from the passive physical form (Snowber, 2018).18

Applying the Aristotelian philosophical concept of phronesis (practical wisdom) to choreography, scholars argue that dance-making, remarkably akin to moral action, relies entirely on the experientially particular rather than universal precepts or abstract generalizations (Carr, 1999).19 It is exceptionally rare, and often creatively sterile, for a choreographer to create a dance in strict, pre-meditated accordance with a theory defined in advance; rather, choreography involves a highly aleatory, creative approach fundamentally entangled with the physical articulation of feeling, weight, and spatial relationships (Carr, 1999).19The application of phronesis suggests that the very act of making dance implicates, unfolds, and cultivates the self, aggressively rejecting the "cool objectivism" demanded by traditional scientific or purely theoretical inquiries (Carr, 1999).19

Furthermore, critical performance studies scholars argue that purely theoretical approaches commit a form of epistemological violence against the dancer. As Tami Spry asserts, "When the body is erased in the process(ing) of scholarship, knowledge situated within the body is unavailable" (Spry, 2001).20 Traditional phenomenology, such as the foundational work of Husserl or Merleau-Ponty, while emphasizing human experience, often does so in a "purely theoretical way" that paradoxically ignores the direct, immediate, and lived, sweating aspects of the actual body (Varela et al., 1993).20 Practice-as-research in dance deliberately seeks to re-emphasize direct embodied experience over theoretical acknowledgment, ensuring that kinesthetic empathy and visceral physical impacts are traced rather than erased (Spry, 2001).20

This fiercely embodied methodology yields a distinct type of academic output, often termed "research-creation" in Canadian institutional contexts (Gilmour, 2021).22 Instead of producing dry, analytical white papers that distance the audience from the cultural subject, practice-based outcomes—such as immersive films documenting Tibetan ritual dance—provide a visceral, sensory experience of being immersed in a community, prioritizing an embodied approach to knowing over an analytical knowing that favors pure thought (Gilmour, 2021).22 Furthermore, by utilizing dance-based methods, researchers can explore complex social and political topics through a highly equitable lens. For example, studies utilizing the embodied cultural resources of dance as "funds of knowledge" for Black girls in physics and STEM learning environments demonstrate how physical interaction can challenge ideological separations that position science as disembodied, emotionless, and purely objective, and art as frivolous and non-cognitive (Annamma et al., 2019).23 In these groundbreaking paradigms, the body is an active, respected agent of scientific and artistic inquiry, generating Nelson's "liquid knowing" in real-time.

## Legitimizing the Purely Theoretical-Conceptual Approach in Dance Studies

Despite the incredibly compelling, phenomenologically rich arguments for centering the moving body and practice-based methodologies, the assertion that dance can onlybe authentically or ethically studied through studio practice is academically reductive. There exist highly specific conditions, complex research paradigms, and urgent socio-historical questions under which a purely theoretical-conceptual approach—aligning perfectly with Borgdorff’s "research on the arts"—is not only fully justified but academically superior for yielding certain vital types of systemic knowledge.

A purely theoretical approach to dance is fundamentally justified when the research seeks to uncover the macro-level structures—historical, sociological, political, and philosophical—that govern how dancing bodies are regulated, perceived, commodified, and institutionalized over time. When the scholarly inquiry moves beyond the individual practitioner's localized phenomenological experience to address the vast systemic forces operating upon the art form, the analytical distance provided by rigorous theoretical frameworks becomes an indispensable critical tool.

### Condition 1: Historical and Structural Analysis (Janet Adshead-Lansdale)

Historically, dance struggled mightily to gain legitimacy in the Western academy precisely because of a pervasive cultural perception that relegated it to mere physical "entertainment," viewing embodied knowledge with profound incredulity and associating it with irrationality or base appetites (Adshead-Lansdale, 1994; Foster, 2021).16 To counter this "principle of dissolution"—where the ephemeral nature of dance made it inherently vulnerable as a lasting academic discipline—scholars like Janet Adshead-Lansdale pioneered rigorous, purely theoretical frameworks for dance analysis and history (Adshead-Lansdale, 1981).16

Adshead-Lansdale argued forcefully against the romanticized anti-intellectualism that sometimes pervaded dance conservatoires, warning that British fears of theory and interpretive reasoning could not survive the sophisticated challenges of post-structuralist thinking (Adshead-Lansdale, 1997).26 She posited that remaining stubbornly "atheoretical" in the name of preserving pure practice was incredibly dangerous, as it risked embedding the "congealed residues" of previous generations' unexamined biases, prejudices, and common-sense assumptions into the historical and educational record (Adshead-Lansdale, 1997).26 By asserting that all knowledge is inherently partial and that all researchers actively choose their constructs, she established that the purely theoretical study of dance is a vital, necessary mechanism for challenging prevailing orthodoxies and bringing hidden ideological structures to light (Adshead-Lansdale, 1997).26

Under Adshead-Lansdale's framework, the study of dance history and analysis relies heavily on theoretical models drawn from literature, sociology, feminism, and the visual arts (Adshead-Lansdale, 1988).25 This conceptual, textual distance allows the researcher to analyze the intricate structural components of choreography, the shifting interpretations of audiences over centuries, and the socio-political conditions of production without being hopelessly bound to the subjective, localized, and often myopic experience of the contemporary studio practitioner (Adshead-Lansdale, 1995).27 Therefore, a theoretical approach is deeply justified when the primary academic goal is to accurately map the historical trajectory of dance, its complex interaction with broader cultural shifts, and its role in cultural reproduction and legitimation (Thomas, 1995).30

### Condition 2: Choreographic Analysis and the Spatialization of Power (Susan Leigh Foster)

The legitimacy of the purely theoretical approach was radically expanded in the 1980s with the advent of "choreographic analysis," a potent methodology most prominently developed by Susan Leigh Foster in her seminal 1986 book, Reading Dancing: Bodies and Subjects in Contemporary American Dance (Foster, 1986).31 Foster was among the very first to rigorously apply post-structuralist theory to dance research, brilliantly transforming choreography from merely a physical object of study into a sophisticated analytical method itself (Foster, 1986).31

Foster’s theoretical approach demonstrated conclusively that dancing bodies are not "natural" entities operating in a vacuum, but are deeply, physically constructed by cultural practices, institutional habits, and verbal/non-verbal discourses (Foster, 1995).33 She categorized the formation of "dancing bodily consciousness" and analyzed precisely how rigorous training regimens (such as classical ballet technique) physically inscribe rigid societal norms, gender roles, and power dynamics directly into the dancer's musculature and bone structure (Foster, 1997).35 Under this theoretical lens, choreography becomes a critical mechanism to access, organize, and destabilize political, structuralist, and post-colonial inquiries (Giersdorf & Wong, 2019).31

Affordances of the Theoretical-Choreographic Approach: The conditions that explicitly justify utilizing choreographic analysis as a purely theoretical methodology include any research aimed at uncovering the "spatialization of power" (Bench, 2023).31 By objectively observing what arranged, planned, and executed movement achieves in a given space, theoretical researchers can illuminate how systemic power (in a distinctly Foucauldian sense) is temporalized, spatialized, and corporealized (Bench, 2023).31 This approach is essential when researching how marginalized bodies negotiate agency through movement, foregrounding the invisible societal forces that constrain, accelerate, or direct physical expression.31Furthermore, analyzing dance as a readable "text" provides a robust avenue to understand not just whatdances mean, but how they actively produce meaning through complex systems of signs and representations (Foster, 1986).31 By treating the dancing body as a socially constructed text, researchers can interface dance studies seamlessly with critical theory, gender studies, Marxist analysis of value exchange (viewing dance as both commodity and gift), and cultural studies without needing to step foot in a studio (Foster, 2021).25

Limitations and Epistemological Caveats: However, the ongoing legitimacy of this theoretical approach is entirely contingent upon researchers acknowledging its inherent, structural limitations. As critics astutely note, choreographic analysis is not a neutral, universal methodology; it emerged specifically from academic circles in the Global North—spaces that have been historically patriarchal, colonial, and capitalist (Perillo, 2023).31 The attempt to translate rich, somatic, physical experiences entirely into the written word often imposes Western epistemological concepts onto practices that may actively resist such textual categorization, resulting in a phenomenon known as the "coloniality of language" (Perillo, 2023).31 This is particularly evident in Asian institutional contexts, where the use of English as the official research language inevitably extends a quasi-colonialism as Western neoliberal research imperatives force specific definitions of "knowledge" onto indigenous movement practices (Chen, 2022).1

Furthermore, theoretical approaches can easily fall prey to privileging "Choreography" (with a capital C)—the elite, codified concert dance traditions of ballet and modern dance—while subtly marginalizing vernacular, social, or street dancing as somehow less analytically "meaningful" or worthy of rigorous study (Savigliano, 2023).31 Therefore, the purely theoretical approach is justified only when the researcher maintains a hyper-vigilant, critical reflexivity regarding the limits of textual translation, avoids the trap of complete disembodiment, and actively resists the imposition of Western conceptual frameworks onto non-Western physical practices (Spry, 2001).20

### Condition 3: Philosophical, Phenomenological, and Meta-Analytical Inquiry

A third major condition justifying a purely theoretical approach occurs within the highly abstract realm of philosophy, specifically within the sub-disciplines of phenomenology and somaesthetics. Scholars utilizing these frameworks do not necessarily engage in dance practice to conduct their research; rather, they apply established philosophical tools to the conceptand observation of dance to generate profound insights about human consciousness, existence, and perception (Bresnahan, 2014).34

As an academic field, the philosophy of dance encompasses metaphysics, epistemology, ethical theory, and the philosophy of mind (Bresnahan, 2014).34 A purely theoretical analysis of dance can profoundly enrich our understanding of embodiment itself, providing unique, rigorous insights into what it means for humans to be complex "body-subjects"—entities that are both body knowers and body-expressers (Block & Kissell, 2001).40 Phenomenological approaches, such as those building upon Husserlian meditations, use the intense observation of dance to reject both strict metaphysical realism (the idea that the world exists entirely mind-independently) and traditional idealism, exploring instead how reality and meaning are actively co-constituted by the subjective, moving body and the observing consciousness (Zahavi, 2003).41 Similarly, the application of Richard Shusterman’s concept of "somaesthetics" provides a robust theoretical vocabulary for understanding our embodied engagement with art, entirely without requiring the researcher to physically perform the movements under study (Shusterman, 2014).34

Furthermore, purely theoretical approaches are utterly indispensable when addressing meta-philosophical and ontological questions, such as "What exactly counts as dance?" (McFee, 1992).34 Delineating the strict boundaries between art-dance, sports-like competitive dancing (e.g., Olympic figure skating or gymnastics), and digital/animated dance requires a level of conceptual synthesis, historical knowledge, and logical argumentation that simply cannot be resolved solely through physical practice in a studio (McFee, 1992).34 Additionally, resolving complex debates surrounding "kinesthetic empathy"—such as the clash between Susan Leigh Foster's assertion that empathy is a socially mediated, contextual construct versus Maxine Sheets-Johnstone's argument that it is an innate, visceral phenomenological reality—requires the deep theoretical synthesis of cognitive science, post-structuralism, and evolutionary biology (Foster, 2011; Sheets-Johnstone, 2009).21

### Condition 4: Sociological, Educational, and Evaluative Contexts

Finally, purely theoretical and conceptual approaches are entirely justified, and frequently demanded, within the contexts of educational policy, sociological evaluation, and the structuring of pedagogical models. For instance, when researchers investigate the systemic impact of dance education on student wellbeing, social cohesion, or "physical literacy" (PL), practice-based methods are insufficient for addressing the macro-level requirements of policy-makers (Pérez-Pueyo et al., 2024).42

In the field of Sport for Development (SFD) and physical education, establishing shared vocabularies, standardized evaluation frameworks, and theoretical-conceptual models is crucial for securing funding, training educators, and measuring long-term impact (Bauer, 2022).42 While practitioners may rightly champion the "immeasurable nature of the lived dance experience," educational policy-makers inevitably privilege robust quantitative data, rigorous evaluation research, and clearly articulated conceptual frameworks (Bonbright & Faber, 2004).42Therefore, theoretical research that translates the localized benefits of dance into structural pedagogical models—such as the integration of Physical Literacy into school curricula—is a perfectly legitimate, necessary application of "research on the arts" that operates entirely outside the boundaries of PaR (Pérez-Pueyo et al., 2024).42

### Evaluating the Matrix: When to Deploy Theory vs. Practice

The decision to utilize a purely theoretical approach rather than a practice-based approach in dance studies ultimately hinges on the specific alignment of the core research question, the desired epistemological outcome, and the methodological fit required to generate valid, verifiable answers (Chilton & Leavy, 2014).45

## The Dialectic of Disciplines: Synthesizing Methodological Paradigms

The perceived hostility and rigid dichotomy between the purely theoretical-conceptual approach and the practice-based approach is, ultimately, an institutional artifact rather than an absolute philosophical necessity. As Henk Borgdorff astutely identifies, the frequent, impassioned pleas for convergence between artistic and academic research are stark, lamentable reflections of the historical schism between those two spheres of human activity (Borgdorff, 2012).3 The ongoing evolution of arts-based methodologies clearly demonstrates that the most robust, groundbreaking scholarship often occurs precisely at the messy intersection of these paradigms.

Robin Nelson’s assertion that PaR inherently involves the "imbrications of theory within practice" provides a powerful synthesis that respects both the physical, kinesthetic intelligence of the dancer and the analytical, structural rigor of the academic (Nelson, 2013).2 However, the existence of this synthesis does not in any way negate the independent validity and necessity of the purely theoretical approach. The theoretical-conceptual mode remains absolutely vital because it provides the overarching critical apparatus that holds localized practice accountable to broader history, society, and philosophy.

While researchers like Spry rightfully caution that erasing the sweating, breathing body from scholarship actively erases knowledge (Spry, 2001) 20, formidable theorists like Adshead-Lansdale and Foster have proven conclusively that writing about the body does not necessitate erasing it. Instead, robust theoretical frameworks like choreographic analysis translate the ephemeral, kinetic energy of dance into a durable, transmissible discourse that can interact with, challenge, and expand the wider humanities (Adshead-Lansdale, 1997; Foster, 1986).26

### Systemic Implications and Future Trajectories

The formal academic acceptance of these varying methodologies has profound, ongoing ripple effects on the administration of higher education, the distribution of funding models, and the future of pedagogy. If practice-based methodologies are to be fully legitimized as equal to theoretical ones—as Borgdorff vehemently demands—institutions must continue to rapidly adjust their auditing mechanisms and definitions of evidence. The traditional textual thesis must make permanent room for Nelson's multi-mode submissions, where the "artwork is the substantial evidence" supported by complementary writing, acknowledging that liquid knowing is just as valuable as hard facts (Nelson, 2013).2

Conversely, the stubborn persistence and continued refinement of the purely theoretical approach in dance studies ensures that the discipline is not entirely siloed into inward-looking, conservatoire-style enclaves. By maintaining rigorous textual, sociological, and historical methodologies, dance studies retains its legibility, authority, and influence among broader academic disciplines. This theoretical bridge allows profound insights derived from the dancing body to directly inform sociology, cognitive science, evolutionary biology, and cultural studies, proving that dance is not merely a physical activity, but a central mechanism for understanding the human condition (Bresnahan, 2014).34

## Conclusion

The epistemological frameworks meticulously established by Henk Borgdorff and Robin Nelson have successfully dismantled the antiquated, damaging notion that art-making is an entirely mysterious, anti-intellectual endeavor incapable of producing valid academic knowledge. Borgdorff’s taxonomy, culminating in "research in the arts," and Nelson’s "Practice as Research" (PaR) model formalize the rigorous mechanisms by which tacit, embodied "know-how" is translated into verifiable "knowing." They establish beyond doubt that material thinking, physical experimentation, and creative praxis are indispensable methodologies for generating new insights that textual abstraction alone simply cannot access.

However, the specific, high-stakes application of these frameworks to dance studies reveals that practice-based methodologies are not the exclusive, nor always the superior, route to truth within the discipline. A purely theoretical-conceptual approach is entirely legitimate—and indeed urgently required—under specific conditions where the research seeks to historicize the art form, analyze the spatialization of societal power, construct pedagogical models, or explore the metaphysical realities of human embodiment. Through the pioneering work of scholars who established dance analysis and choreographic analysis, the theoretical approach has proven exceptionally capable of reading the dancing body as a complex, culturally inscribed text. Ultimately, the epistemological health of artistic research relies not on the supremacy of one method over the other, but on a pluralistic, dynamic ecosystem where the visceral immediacy of practice and the critical distance of theory operate in continuous, productive dialectical tension.
