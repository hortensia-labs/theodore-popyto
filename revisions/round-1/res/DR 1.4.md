**Key arguments (200–300 words)**  
Sheets-Johnstone’s core claim—developed across her dance phenomenology and her later philosophy of life and mind—is that movement is not a secondary expression of cognition, but a ground condition of it: *thinking is modeled on the body*, and meaningful intelligibility is rooted in animate, sensorimotor life rather than in disembodied symbolic operations. In *The Primacy of Movement*, she explicitly targets Western mind/body dualism and argues that “remarkable mental adaptations” should be understood as grounded in “animate form,” with conceptual patterns (e.g., analogical thinking) arising from corporeal-kinetic experience rather than from a “rarified mental essence.” citeturn41view0turn41view1 This emphasis pushes phenomenology beyond “embodiment” as slogan: consciousness is treated as fundamentally corporeal, tethered to tactile–proprioceptive–kinesthetic sensitivities and evolutionary forms of responsiveness. citeturn41view1turn69view0  

In *The Corporeal Turn*, she intensifies the critique of mechanistic metaphors. The “simile of a machine,” she argues, reduces the body to a third-person object “devoid of intentionality” and affective power, encouraging a technologically oriented stance toward bodily life; by contrast, she defends the irreducibility of first-person bodily presence as the primordial “center and origin of our being in the world,” prior to scientific-technological re-description. citeturn43view0turn43view2 This maps strongly onto the Husserlian distinction between *Leib* (lived body) and *Körper* (body as object), where objectification is derivative of prereflective bodily life. citeturn20search0  

Finally, *The Phenomenology of Dance* is widely treated as a classic because it establishes a rigorous descriptive program for dance as lived experience—insisting that analysis must repeatedly return to the “wholeness” of the danced phenomenon, and foregrounding kinesthesia (often neglected in favor of proprioception). Sheets-Johnstone gives you a philosophically precise vocabulary for showing why AI’s treatment of movement as data (trajectories, parameters, style tokens) risks collapsing *Leib* into *Körper*. Your “embodied resistance” claim can be sharpened by her insistence that movement’s meaning is grounded in *actual kinesthetic experience*—not merely in visualizable outcomes or brain-localizable “happenings.” citeturn69view1turn41view1 When dancers reject, rework, or “cannot take up” an AI-generated phrase, Sheets-Johnstone helps you argue that the resistance is not simply aesthetic preference but a phenomenological mismatch: the proposal fails to *solicit* lived motility, qualitative dynamics, and kinesthetic memory in a way that becomes inhabitable. **Key arguments (200–300 words)**  
In *Choreographing Empathy*, Foster’s central intervention is to challenge the assumption of a “direct psychophysical connection” between dancer and spectator. A key consequence is that “kinesthetic empathy” is neither a simple universal mechanism nor a purely private, ineffable feeling; it is produced at the intersection of (1) choreographic propositions (what a work offers as a kinesthetic invitation), (2) spectators’ embodied histories and situated nervous systems, and (3) cultural techniques for interpreting and narrating bodily sensation. Foster’s genealogy explicitly contextualizes canonical modernist claims about spectators’ bodily “metakinesis,” including those of entity["people","John Martin","dance critic 1893-1985"], by exposing how race, class, and gender assumptions have shaped the “ideal” empathic observer in dance criticism. citeturn30view0turn31view1

**Relevance to embodied resistance to AI automation**  
For a dissertation on AI-generated movement, Foster helps you avoid a common trap: treating spectators’ responses as automatic “mirror” effects that AI might eventually replicate. Her framework encourages you to treat empathic response as choreographically and culturally *made*, not merely triggered. citeturn31view0turn30view0 This strengthens an “embodied resistance” argument in two ways: first, it explains why AI outputs can be legible yet fail to produce felt resonance—because resonance depends on historically situated kinesthetic literacies and on choreographic address, not just on kinematic plausibility. **Key arguments (200–300 words)**  
The Calvo-Merino “action observation” studies are widely cited because they empirically demonstrate that perceiving dance is not only a visual-cognitive act but is modulated by an observer’s *motor repertoire*—a finding often mobilized in discussions of kinesthetic empathy and motor resonance. In the landmark 2005 fMRI study, the authors compared expert dancers (classical ballet, capoeira) with non-expert controls while participants watched ballet or capoeira actions. The logic is explicitly repertoire-based: comparing brain activity when experts watch movements they are trained to perform versus movements they are not trained to perform reveals the influence of motor expertise. The study reports greater activations in premotor and parietal regions (including intraparietal sulcus) and other areas when experts view movements from their own trained style, supporting the conclusion that the “mirror system” integrates observed action with an individual’s personal motor repertoire and that the brain “understands actions by motor simulation.” citeturn23view0turn23view1  

The 2006 “Seeing or doing?” report sharpens the argument by dissociating visual familiarity from motor familiarity through a clever stimulus design: professional ballet dancers of different genders view gender-specific versus gender-common ballet moves. Because male and female dancers are visually familiar with both types of moves (they train together), increased activation for own-gender motor repertoire cannot be explained by mere visual familiarity. The authors report evidence for a “purely motoric” influence on action observation and argue that action understanding is not only visual recognition but also motoric; they also highlight cerebellar involvement in the observation network. citeturn24view0turn24view1  

The 2010 paper extends the expertise account to perception: it frames biological motion perception as influenced by familiarity and investigates how visual/motor experience modulates configural processing of actions. citeturn28search0

**Relevance to embodied resistance to AI automation**  
These studies can function as a carefully bounded empirical support for your phenomenological claim that spectatorship is not reducible to external kinematics (a Körper-like view of bodies as observable objects). Instead, what is perceived is partly shaped by the observer’s lived motor history: a Leib-grounded repertoire that conditions what “makes sense” as movement. citeturn23view0turn24view0 For generative AI, this has two direct implications: (1) AI-generated movement can be visually coherent yet fail to recruit embodied motor resonance in trained viewers, producing a felt resistance (estrangement, “uninhabitability,” or mismatch) that your phenomenological framework can analyze; and (2) dancers’ embodied expertise becomes a site of critique against automation, because expertise is precisely what differentiates “seeing” from “understanding” in these studies’ terms.
